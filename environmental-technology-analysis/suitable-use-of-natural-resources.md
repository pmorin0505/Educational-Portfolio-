Suitable Use of Natural Resources and Environmental Services

One of the core ideas of sustainability is the suitable use of Earth’s natural resources and 
environmental services. This principle means that we should use natural resources at a rate that 
doesn't go past the Earth’s ability to regenerate them and must avoid overwhelming 
environmental systems that deal with waste and pollution. Natural resources such as forests, 
freshwater, and soil provide the materials necessary for survival. Things like climate regulation 
and water filtration support life, even if we don’t always notice it. When our society consumes 
resources faster than they can replenish or produces waste faster than ecosystems can dispose of 
it, the system stops working.

One way to think about this is here on a college campus at Bridgewater State University, 
shared facilities are maintained because everyone depends on them. If items like paper towels in 
the bathrooms are used but never replaced, the system fails. Students will start using other things 
to dry their hands, and in this situation people will start turning to different materials. It 
snowballs from there. Another analogy is dining halls offering reusable containers that should be 
returned. It reflects a closed-loop model of use and return (reduce, reuse, recycle). Sustainability 
functions the same way. The Earth is not an infinite supplier of materials, yet unfortunately we 
often treat its natural systems as infinite.

One clear example of overusing resources against the suitable use of natural resources is 
through deforestation and material consumption.Around 10 million hectares of forest are lost 
each year (Food and Agriculture Organization [FAO], 2020). Forests provide timber and paper, 
but they also help control climate, store carbon, and prevent soil from washing away. Much of 
the wood harvested today is used for short-lived products such as cardboard packaging, 
disposable paper goods, and shipping materials. For example, the rapid growth of e-commerce 
has dramatically increased cardboard production. While cardboard is in theory recyclable, large 
amounts still end up in landfills due to contamination, improper sorting, or overproduction.

The problem isn't that we just use trees, it is that they are being used inefficiently and 
wastefully. A sheet of paper used for a temporary document such as a receipt, packaging slip, or 
short-term notice may be thrown out within days or weeks. When multiplied across billions of 
transactions worldwide, this represents enormous resource depletion for minimal long-term 
benefit. According to ScienceDirect (2024), paper and paperboard products still make up a 
significant portion of municipal solid waste in the United States, even though recycling programs 
exist. The principle of suitable use requires not just recycling but reduction and responsible 
consumption.

Another significant problem is the production of synthetic, non-biodegradable materials. 
Today companies rely on plastics obtained from fossil fuels. Plastics such as polystyrene 
(styrene-based products) are durable and inexpensive, but they do not break down easily. Instead, 
they fragment into microplastics, which contaminate soil, waterways, oceans, animals, food, and 
even human bloodstreams (Beyond Plastics, 2026). Unlike natural materials such as wood or 
cotton, which re-enter biological cycles with minimal long-term harm, synthetic plastics will 
exist for centuries.

I believe the introduction of artificial materials has worsened the sustainability crisis. 
Ecosystems evolved to process organic matter, not petroleum-based polymers. The Earth can 
regenerate forests over time, but it can't naturally break down plastics at the rate we produce 
them. Global plastic production now exceeds 400 million metric tons per year (Our World in 
Data, 2026), way more than our waste management can handle. This clearly goes against the 
principle of suitable use, as we're producing more waste than nature can break down.

Corporate practices also contribute to unsustainable resource use. Many companies 
design products with limited lifespans. As someone who works at Best Buy, I constantly see 
printer ink cartridges being purchased. The cartridges are frequently difficult to refill or recycle. 
Consumers must purchase replacements, generating continuous waste. While some 
manufacturers offer “recycling” programs, participation rates remain low, and many cartridges 
end up in landfills. This model prioritizes profit over long-term resource stewardship.

When you really think about it you'll see retail environments like Walmart or TJ Maxx 
further demonstrate the scale of resource inefficiency. Large stores stock vast quantities of goods 
clothing, décor, electronics, and seasonal items that require energy, water, and raw materials to 
produce. Unsold inventory is often thrown out. The fashion industry is responsible for 
approximately 10% of global carbon emissions and significant water pollution. When products 
are manufactured quickly, consumed briefly, and disposed of rapidly, natural resource cycles are 
disrupted (Netflix, 2024).

The historical case of Easter Island provides a powerful illustration of what happens 
when a society fails to use resources sustainably. Easter Island (Rapa Nui) is one of the most 
geographically isolated inhabited places on Earth. As Kaufmann (2007) explains, its isolation 
created a closed environmental system with minimal external influence. The island’s inhabitants 
relied entirely on local forests, soil, and underwater resources. Over time, large-scale 
deforestation occurred, partly due to agricultural expansion and the construction of massive stone 
statues known as moai.

As trees disappeared, soil erosion increased, crop declined, and the ability to build sturdy 
canoes decreased. Without canoes, exploration decreased and fishing practices ceased to exist, 
further reducing food supply. Archaeological evidence suggests that population decline and 
social instability followed (Diamond, 2005). Because the island lacked fossil fuels, industrial 
technology, or synthetic materials, the environmental degradation was directly linked to human 
decision-making and overuse of natural resources.

Easter Island acts as a warning. It demonstrates that even without modern pollution, a 
society can collapse if it exceeds its ecological limits. However, modern civilization introduces 
additional problems. Unlike the islanders, modern society uses fossil fuels, produces synthetic 
waste, and operates on global supply chains. Climate change, biodiversity loss, and ocean 
acidification are results of resource use on a planetary scale (ScienceDirect, 2023). If a small, 
isolated society could destabilize itself through deforestation alone, the risks facing a global 
industrial civilization are far greater.

Some might argue that modern technology and global trade prevent collapse by allowing 
societies to import resources when local supplies trend downward. This shifts environmental 
burdens geographically rather than resolving them. The Earth as a whole remains a closed 
system. There is no external planet from which to import additional forests, freshwater, or 
atmospheric capacity to absorb carbon emissions.

The principle of suitable use does not require stopping all production or consumption. 
Instead, it requires aligning human activity with ecological limits. Renewable energy adoption, 
circular manufacturing systems, biodegradable material innovation, and stronger recycling 
infrastructure are steps in the right direction. However, these efforts must be backed by cultural 
change. Consumers must question whether products are necessary, durable, and responsibly 
produced. Corporations must prioritize long-term environmental stability over short-term profit.

I believe the suitable use of natural resources and environmental services demands that 
societies consume resources responsibly and minimize waste beyond ecological capacity. Today, 
we go against this principle through deforestation, plastic production, corporate waste practices, 
and excessive consumption. The collapse of Easter Island illustrates the dangers of ignoring 
environmental limits. Our global civilization faces even greater risks due to industrial pollution 
and synthetic materials. Sustainability requires recognizing that Earth isn't an unlimited 
warehouse of materials but a shared home with finite capacity. If societies continue to exceed 
regenerative limits, environmental decline will accelerate more rapidly than historical precedents 
suggest. The principle of suitable use isn't at all theoretical it is essential for long-term human 
survival.
